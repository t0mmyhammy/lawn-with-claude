# Example Plan — Zone 6a, Kentucky Bluegrass (Metro Detroit)

This is a fully worked example so you can see what the AI produces. It's a real
cool-season program for a Kentucky bluegrass lawn in USDA zone 6a (metro Detroit:
last frost early-to-mid May, first frost mid-October). Yours will differ based on
your soil test, size, and stores. Treat this as an illustration, not a
prescription, and always read the label.

## Profile this plan was built from
- Zone 6a, metro Detroit. ~4,000 sq ft, mostly full sun, shade under one maple.
- Kentucky bluegrass. Condition: decent but thin in spots, some crabgrass history.
- Irrigation: in-ground sprinklers. Program: hybrid (lean synthetic, open to
  organic). Buys at Costco + Home Depot, occasional local nursery.
- Soil test ([MySoil kit](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B084TSNR79), ~$30): pH 6.4,
  low phosphorus, potassium adequate.
- Goal: thicker, kid-and-pet-friendly, a few hours a month.

## The number that anchors everything
For Kentucky bluegrass, **fall is the season that matters most.** Spring is for
crabgrass prevention and a light feed. The real thickening, the overseeding and
the heavy feeding, happens late August through November. Get fall right and the
rest is maintenance.

## Season calendar

**Early spring — soil temp ~55F (forsythia in bloom, ~mid-April)**
- Apply pre-emergent for crabgrass. Active ingredient: **prodiamine** (longer
  lasting) or pendimethalin. Timing beats everything here: too late and crabgrass
  is already up.
- Do NOT overseed now if you used pre-emergent. They cancel each other out.
- First mow once it's actively growing; clean up debris.

**Late spring (May)**
- Spot-treat broadleaf weeds (dandelion, clover) only where present. Active
  ingredient: a **2,4-D / triclopyr** blend for synthetic, or hand-pull / corn
  gluten for organic.
- Light nitrogen feed if the lawn is pale. Keep it modest; heavy spring N feeds
  top growth and weakens summer roots.

**Summer (June-August) — survival mode, not growth mode**
- Raise the mower. Kentucky bluegrass should be cut at **3 to 3.5 inches** in
  summer. Taller blades shade the soil and starve weeds.
- Water **deep and infrequent**: ~1 to 1.5 inches per week including rain,
  early morning. Measure with a tuna can. Frequent shallow watering builds weak
  roots.
- Grub watch: if you've had grubs before, a preventive in late June. Active
  ingredient: **chlorantraniliprole** (Acelepryn) is lower-toxicity and
  pollinator-friendlier than imidacloprid.
- Minimal to no nitrogen in peak heat.

**Late summer / early fall (late Aug-Sept) — THE big window**
- **Core aerate** if soil is compacted, then **overseed** with a quality KBG or
  KBG/fine-fescue blend. Seed-to-soil contact is everything.
- **Starter fertilizer** at seeding. Because the soil test showed low phosphorus,
  a starter with P is justified and legal here (Michigan restricts P unless a
  test shows need or you're establishing seed). Active ingredients: balanced
  starter, e.g. a 12-12-12-style or labeled starter fertilizer.
- Keep new seed consistently moist (light, frequent) until germination, then back
  off to deep-and-infrequent.

**Fall (Sept-Nov) — feed, feed, feed**
- Primary nitrogen feeding in September. Active ingredient: slow-release N
  (synthetic 24-0-6-style, or **Milorganite** for the organic-leaning option).
- **Dormant / late-fall feeding** late October into early November, just before
  the ground freezes. This is the single most valuable feeding of the year for
  cool-season grass: it goes to roots, not top growth, and greens you up early
  next spring.
- Lower the final mow slightly and mulch the last leaves into the turf.

## Shopping list by store

**Home Depot (consumables + tools)**
- Pre-emergent (prodiamine-based) — spring
- Broadleaf weed control (2,4-D/triclopyr) — spot use
- Fall nitrogen fertilizer (slow-release) + starter fertilizer for overseed
- Broadcast spreader (one-time), tuna can / rain gauge (one-time)

**Costco (bulk value when in season)**
- Grass seed (buy a KBG-appropriate blend; check the tag for weed-seed %)
- Milorganite or comparable slow-release fert, if stocked

**Local nursery / MSU Extension**
- Soil test kit (mail-in, ~$20) — do this first
- Regionally-suited KBG/fescue blend if Costco's is generic
- Lime only if a future test shows pH dropping below ~6.0

Rough first-year cost: a few hundred dollars including the one-time spreader,
versus the $50-80/month service. The savings compound once the tools are bought.

## Watering and mowing quick reference
- Water: ~1-1.5 in/week incl. rain, early AM, deep and infrequent.
- Mow: 3-3.5 in summer, never remove more than 1/3 of the blade at once, mulch
  clippings to recycle nitrogen.

## What this plan will not do
It won't aerate the soil, drag the spreader, pull the weeds it tells you to pull,
or fix the sprinkler head that breaks in April. The plan is the brain. The work
is yours. That was always the deal.
